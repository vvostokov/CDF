# What Surprised Us

> Things that did not match our model of the world.

Surprises are valuable: they reveal where our model was incomplete or wrong. Each surprise is an opportunity to **update the model**.

## What Goes Here

Each entry has:

* **Date** — when the surprise was observed.
* **Expectation** — what we expected to happen.
* **Reality** — what actually happened.
* **Difference** — what specifically was wrong about the expectation.
* **Source of surprise** — which part of our model was incorrect.
* **Model update** — what should change in the model as a result.
* **Pattern?** — has this kind of surprise happened before?

## The Purpose of This File

Two purposes:

1. **Update the model.** Each surprise is an opportunity to revise a claim, a domain model, or a confidence score.
2. **Calibrate epistemics.** Repeated surprises in the same area suggest the team's confidence was miscalibrated.

## Calibration Implications

When a surprise occurs:

* What was the confidence **before** the surprise?
* What is the confidence **after**?
* Is the gap consistent with the evidence?

If the gap is consistently large (high confidence before, frequent surprises), the team is over-confident in that area. This is logged in `cdf-improvement-log.md` and addressed in epistemics.
